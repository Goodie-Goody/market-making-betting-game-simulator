The question

Can a market maker earn the spread without being run over?

A market maker quotes a bid and an ask at the same time, hoping to earn the difference as others trade against both sides. Three forces work against that — and the interesting question is not "does market-making work" but which quoting rule holds up, and when.

The three risks

Inventory risk

Fills are random, so you drift into a net long or short position. While you hold it, the price moves — an unwanted directional bet.

Adverse selection

Some counterparties know more than you. Informed flow trades with you only when it profits them — exactly when it costs you.

Execution risk

Quote too wide and you rarely trade; quote too tight and you trade constantly at thin edge. Fill rate falls as you widen.

The model

The mid-price follows a random walk and settles at a terminal value, so held inventory is genuine P&L risk. The key ingredient the original exercise lacked is a fill model: a quote posted a distance δ from the mid is filled with probability

P(fill | δ) = min(1, e−k·δ)

Fills decay exponentially as you widen — so quoting is a real risk–reward trade-off, not a free lunch. A fraction of arrivals are informed: they know the settlement value and trade only when it hurts you, which is what makes the flow toxic.

Policies compared

Naive symmetric

Fixed spread around the mid. No inventory awareness — the control case.

Linear inventory skew

Fixed spread, but the mid is shifted against current inventory to lean away from risk.

Avellaneda–Stoikov

Reservation price and optimal spread — both scale with volatility and time remaining.

What it shows

Skewing beats not-skewing by a mile; adaptivity beats static skewing only when the market is genuinely dangerous.

Inventory control is the first-order survival factor — the naive policy collapses under stress. Between the two skewing rules, a simple linear skew is competitive (and wins raw P&L) in calm markets, while Avellaneda–Stoikov earns its extra machinery specifically in the volatile, toxic regime it was designed for, by widening exactly when conditions are worst.

Honest limitations

  • Informed traders know the exact settlement value — a deliberately strong form of adverse selection.
  • The mid is a constant-volatility arithmetic random walk; real markets have jumps and stochastic volatility.
  • One unit per fill; no queue position, latency, or transaction costs.

This is a research simulation with stated simplifications, not a trading system. Every number here is reproducible from the notebook.
